Data Cleansing

Why do we require Data Cleansing?

The benefit of requiring data cleansing is the overall ability to use the data points themselves. Having clean data causes less errors, makes it easier to present, and gives it larger capability to be used. This is done by getting rid of unneeded groups of data, replacing or deleting places were there is no data points that can be used (NaN, n/a, etc.) , and getting only the needed data points.

Steps involved:

  • The first step in our data cleansing process was dropping any unwanted columns, such as the unnamed column because there was no valuable data, the country code because the continental region and country names could adequately signify geographical location, as well as the average Mbps, as more than 50% of the data values in that column were NaN.

  • Our second step of the data cleansing process involved deleting all data that contained null values. This being columns where there was valuable data but still a small amount of rows that had null values that were not necessary to present in our diagrams as to state them as null and taking up key space in the diagrams.

  • In the third step of data cleansing we had to transform column data types from strings to floats and integers by first removing commas and non-numerical values as to be able to convert them and make it easier to use in later parts when displaying it in the diagrams.

  • The final step included deleting any other columns that would not be helpful and presentable in the diagrams and creating new ones as to help create data points that could be used for later such as the Mbps and creating the percentage column.

  • The five most expensive countries in terms of the average cost of 1GB of mobile data are Malawi, São Tomé and Príncipe, Saint Helena, Ascension and Tristan da Cunha, the Falkland Islands, and Equatorial Guinea. Sub-Saharan Africa is one of the most expensive regions in the world for data, while island nations also tend to be very expensive, which is confirmable because four of the five are located in Sub-Saharan Africa, and three of the five are island nations.

Conclusion:

    • The Northern American region only contains four countries, one of which is Bermuda. Bermuda’s internet cost for 1 gigabyte is 20 USD, which is the reason why the average cost of internet in Northern America is the highest throughout the entire world.

  • Reasons for the high data cost in Sub-Saharan Africa:

    • A significant factor behind the high cost of data in SSA is its lack of infrastructure. With overburdened networks, the data bundles offered in the region are generally smaller. This drives up the average cost per gigabyte when compared to countries with unlimited packages. Another element that contributes to SSA’s high costs is its lack of market competition. In countries with multiple competing networks, such as Nigeria, the cost of data is lower.

  • Asia, Northern Africa, the Baltics, and Eastern Europe offer 1 gigabyte of data for 2.2 USD or less.

  • During COVID-19, there was a rise in digitalization and internet usage from online learning and mandatory quarantines, without a significant increase in cost.

    • The declining entry barriers has been adopted as the standard in this industry in which internet service providers are forced to maintain low to compete in such an environment.

CONCLUSION:

Many of the cheapest countries in which to buy mobile data fall roughly into one of two categories. Some have excellent mobile and fixed broadband infrastructure and so providers are able to offer large amounts of data and internet plans, which brings down the price per gigabyte. Others with less advanced broadband networks are heavily reliant on mobile data and the economy dictates that prices must be low, as that’s what people can afford.

At the more expensive end of the list, we have countries where often the infrastructure isn’t great but also where consumption is very small. People are often buying data packages of just a tens of megabytes at a time, making a gigabyte a relatively large and therefore expensive amount of data to buy. Many countries in the middle of the list have good infrastructure and competitive mobile markets, and while their prices aren’t among the cheapest in the world, they wouldn’t necessarily be considered expensive by its consumers.
